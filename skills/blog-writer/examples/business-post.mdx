## The moment most founders hire too late

The NFIB's 2024 employment survey found that 42% of small business owners reported "few or no qualified applicants" for open positions. But the interesting number is what comes before that: the median solo founder waits 14 months after first hitting capacity before making their first hire.

Fourteen months of turning down work. Fourteen months of doing $15/hour tasks when your time generates $150/hour. That's not being "careful with money." That's leaving money on the table because hiring feels scarier than exhaustion.

The signal isn't "I'm busy." Every founder is busy. The signal is declining work that would grow the business, spending hours on tasks someone else could do for a fraction of your effective rate, or watching growth flatten because you're the bottleneck - not the market.

## Employee vs. contractor isn't about cost

Most founders frame the first hire as an expense question. "Can I afford an employee?" But that's the wrong frame. The real question is: what kind of relationship does this work require?

The IRS has a clear test for this. If you control when, where, and how the work gets done, that's an employee - regardless of what you call them. Misclassifying an employee as a contractor to save on taxes is one of the most common (and most expensive) compliance mistakes small businesses make. Penalties include back taxes, interest, and potential fines.

But beyond compliance, think about growth. A contractor does a job and leaves. An employee learns your business, anticipates problems, and eventually handles things you haven't thought of yet. Your first hire isn't just filling a role - it's adding a second brain to the operation.

<KeyTakeaway title="The real cost of not hiring">
Every hour you spend on $15/hour work is an hour you're not spending on $150/hour work.
If your effective rate is $150/hour and you spend 10 hours a week on admin, shipping,
and customer service, you're paying $1,500/week for tasks a $20/hour hire could handle.
That's $78,000 per year in lost revenue capacity.
</KeyTakeaway>

## The math that makes the decision obvious

Forget gut feelings. Run this calculation.

Take your average monthly revenue over the last 6 months. Multiply by 12 for annual revenue. Now calculate the fully loaded cost of the hire: base salary plus 30% for taxes, insurance, and equipment. If 6 months of current revenue covers the annual fully loaded cost, you can afford the hire.

But "can afford" isn't the right bar. The real question is: will this hire pay for themselves? If the hire frees you to take on two more clients per month at $3,000 each, that's $72,000 in new annual revenue for a $65,000 cost. The hire doesn't cost money. The hire makes money.

<Highlight>
A hire that frees you to generate revenue isn't an expense. It's the highest-ROI
investment your business can make.
</Highlight>

According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, employer costs for employee compensation averaged $46.14 per hour worked in Q3 2024. About 29.4% of that goes to benefits. But for a small business hiring its first employee, the real overhead is closer to 25-30% because you're likely not offering the full benefits package of a Fortune 500 company.

Start simple. Salary, payroll taxes (7.65% FICA), workers' comp insurance (1-3%), and basic equipment. You can add health insurance, retirement matching, and other benefits as the business grows. Don't let the full cost of corporate employment scare you away from hiring at the level that makes sense for where you are now.
