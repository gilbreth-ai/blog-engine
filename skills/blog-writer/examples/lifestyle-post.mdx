## Store-bought coffee is already stale

Here's something the bag doesn't tell you. Coffee peaks between 3 and 10 days after roasting. By the time those "freshly roasted" beans reach the grocery shelf, they're usually 30-90 days old. That's not fresh. That's furniture.

The difference between 5-day-old beans and 60-day-old beans isn't subtle. It's the difference between tasting blueberry and chocolate notes in an Ethiopian natural and tasting... brown. Flat, generic, coffee-flavored coffee.

Home roasting solves this completely. You roast on Saturday, rest the beans overnight, and drink peak-freshness coffee from Monday through the following weekend. Then you roast again. The whole cycle takes 8 minutes of active work.

And the cost? Green (unroasted) beans run $6-8 per pound. A pound of "specialty" roasted beans at the store costs $16-22. You're getting better coffee for less than half the price. The catch is that it's addictive, and you'll start judging every cafe you walk into.

## Your first roast will be terrible (and that's the point)

Don't invest in expensive equipment. Your first 10 roasts are tuition, not product. A hot air popcorn popper from a thrift store is the best beginner roaster ever invented - not because it's precise, but because it's forgiving and cheap.

Why a popcorn popper? The hot air agitates the beans constantly, creating even heat distribution. It reaches the right temperatures (370-450F) naturally. And when you inevitably burn your first batch, you've wasted $0.50 of green beans and zero pride. You learn by wrecking cheap batches, not by reading about roast curves.

A dedicated home roaster like the FreshRoast SR540 ($190) gives you temperature control and a chaff collector. But buy one after your 20th popcorn popper roast, when you know what "first crack" sounds like and what color means "stop now."

## The six minutes that change everything

Roasting coffee is mostly listening and watching. The beans tell you where they are in the process - you just have to pay attention.

  title="The roasting process"

    { title: "Second crack (7+ min)", description: "Quieter, more rapid crackling. The beans are approaching dark roast. Smoke increases significantly. Origin flavors give way to roast flavors - bittersweet chocolate, smokiness. Stop before this unless you specifically want dark roast." },

The most common beginner mistake is panicking at first crack and dumping the beans too early. First crack is just the starting line. Give the beans another 30-90 seconds of development after first crack starts, and you'll land in a sweet medium roast that has both origin character and roast depth.

The second most common mistake is not cooling fast enough. Beans hold heat. If you dump them into a bowl and walk away, they'll continue roasting for another 30-60 seconds from residual heat. Transfer them to a metal colander immediately and toss or fan them until they're cool to the touch.
